### Evidence notes — juspay/hyperswitch, axis 1 (Production proof): Flowbird and Narvar case studies

**Source and version.** The figures below were read first-hand from a **Wayback Machine (web.archive.org) archive** of the original hyperswitch.io case-studies page, "Flowbird leverages Hyperswitch as strategic partner to solve for global parking payments," accessed directly in a browser session on 2026-07-14. The current live URL (`hyperswitch.io/case-studies/flowbird-leverages-hyperswitch-as-strategic-partner-to-solve-for-global-parking-payments`) returns an **S3 AccessDenied** error and could not be reached directly — the archive was the only route to first-hand content.

**Verbatim quotes (Wayback archive of the original hyperswitch.io page):**
- Payment volume: "350 million plus payment transactions every year"
- UX: "the average number of clicks required to complete a payment was reduced significantly, from 10 to just 3"
- Reliability: "reliable, scalable, agile, and high-performing, ensuring 99.999% uptime"
- Scale: "a cache-based shock absorber to manage peak traffic, scaling seamlessly up to 20,000 TPS"

Also stated on the same archived page (paraphrased, not verbatim): 7 million users; 6,000 cities across 80 countries; multi-region active-active deployment; PCI DSS compliance; Flowbird's own card-present system ("Archipel") self-integrated as a connector into Hyperswitch. As of the original 2026-07-14 check, the same archived page also named other adopters via case-study links/logos — Recurly, Zurich, ShopLC, Narvar, and others — with only the Flowbird case study's body text read first-hand at that time; see the 2026-07-17 update below for what has since been confirmed or found to no longer be locatable.

**2026-07-17 update — Narvar read first-hand; Recurly/ShopLC no longer locatable; Zurich logo-only.** Narvar's own case study was reached directly at `juspay.io/customer-stories/narvar` (200 OK, no 404/S3 AccessDenied — no Wayback fallback was needed). Its content is purely qualitative — no transaction-volume, uptime, or TPS figure of any kind is stated. VERBATIM: the page's section heading reads "Frictionless Merchant Scaling" (title case, as a heading — not a sentence in body prose). VERBATIM body text: "it doesn't serve a single merchant – it serves thousands of distinct retail brands." (This entry's earlier draft paraphrased the latter as "across thousands of brands," which is not an exact quote — the wording above is the corrected verbatim text.) This earns Narvar a ★ for "the case study was read and says what it says," but it is a thin ★: there is no quantitative claim to weigh, unlike Flowbird's rich figures above. Separately, a check across the live hyperswitch.io/case-studies page, its Wayback CDX history, and juspay.io/customer-stories' own index (~30 named slugs) found no Recurly or ShopLC entry anywhere — not a single archived snapshot, live page, or guessed-slug attempt (e.g. hyperswitch.io/case-studies/shoplc, /shop-lc) turned one up. **Index-reliability caveat (added after independent review):** the juspay.io/customer-stories index by itself is weak evidence of absence — Narvar's own case study, confirmed reachable above at a working URL, does not appear in that same index either, so a name's absence from the index does not mean its page doesn't exist. The Recurly/ShopLC conclusion here rests on the combination of the Wayback CDX history (which tracks archived URLs, not the live index) and slug-guessing, not on the index check alone. Zurich still appears, but only as a logo in the live page's customer-showcase grid — no case-study link or slug was found for it. This does not mean Recurly/ShopLC/Zurich case studies never existed (the page has visibly been restructured since 2026-07-14 — Flowbird itself moved from case-studies to a "S3 AccessDenied" dead end over the same period), only that they cannot currently be verified as present, so this entry no longer carries them forward as "named on the page, unread."

**Reconciled discrepancy — customer-showcase count.** 021's session counted "~30 named slugs" in the juspay.io/customer-stories index. In independent re-verification, the mothership's own WebFetch of the same page surfaced only 17 customer names (Agoda, Amazon, CRED, Flipkart, Google, HDFC, IndiGo, McDonald's, Microsoft, Myntra, OnePlus, Starbucks, Swiggy, Wego, Xiaomi, Tata Cliq, Star Health Insurance). Neither count is asserted as authoritative here: WebFetch renders pages through a summarizing model, so a logo-wall/grid section and a distinct slug/story-index section could easily be undercounted or conflated depending on which part of the page a given fetch's summary emphasized — this is the same class of fidelity limitation flagged elsewhere in this catalog for WebFetch-mediated reads. Both observations are recorded rather than silently picking one; Agoda appearing in both counts is at least consistent between them. This does not affect the Recurly/ShopLC/Narvar conclusions above, which rest on the Wayback CDX and direct-URL checks rather than on either count of the index.

**Newer, separate first-party source.** A different, current page — `juspay.io/customer-stories/flowbird` — covers the same Flowbird relationship but with more conservative framing: monthly transaction volume of 650,000/month scaling to 1,000,000/month. **The 20,000 TPS figure, the 99.999% uptime figure, and the 10-to-3-clicks figure are not repeated on this newer page.** Readers should treat those three specific numbers as unconfirmed by the current first-party source.

**Required caveats (read before treating this as ★ evidence):**
1. **Source-version caveat:** verification was performed via the Wayback Machine archive of the original hyperswitch.io page; the live page currently returns S3 AccessDenied and was not reachable directly during this check.
2. **Vendor-published caveat:** every figure above is vendor-published (by Hyperswitch/Juspay itself), not an independently audited number. It carries the same limitation as every other case study in this catalog — it is not third-party verification (that remains axis 5, marked ◇ for this entry).
3. **Figure-nature caveat:** "350 million transactions/year" is Flowbird's total payment volume, not necessarily volume processed through Hyperswitch specifically. "20,000 TPS" is a stated scaling capability/ceiling, not a claim of sustained/persistent throughput. "99.999% uptime" is presented as a design/reliability target, not an independently measured/audited uptime statistic. In addition, the newer juspay.io/customer-stories/flowbird page revises the volume figure (650k/month → 1M/month) and omits the 10→3 clicks, 20,000 TPS, and 99.999% uptime figures entirely — treat those three numbers as unconfirmed by the current first-party source.
4. **Scope caveat:** this ★ applies only to the Flowbird and Narvar case studies confirmed above (see the 2026-07-17 update note for Narvar's much thinner, purely-qualitative evidence). Other adopters currently visible on hyperswitch.io/case-studies or juspay.io/customer-stories — Innago, Bask Health, Agoda, and several anonymized entries ("Global Insurance Giant", "Fintech Operator", "Retail Brand", "Ticketing Platform") — have not been read first-hand and remain at ☆. Do not read this entry as confirming production proof for any adopter other than Flowbird and Narvar.
